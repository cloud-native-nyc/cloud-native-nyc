# Cloud Native New York City (NYC) Chapter Governance

## 1. Purpose
This document establishes a transparent framework for organizing Cloud Native NYC. It defines roles, responsibilities, and processes that enable us to:
* Expand our organizing team sustainably
* Distribute work to prevent burnout
* Make decisions fairly and efficiently
* Create a welcoming path for new contributors

## 2. Guiding Principles
* **Community Stewardship:** Organizer roles serve the community, not personal interests.
* **Vendor Neutrality:** No single company dominates; maximum 50% of organizers from the same employer.
* **Decentralized Leadership:** Teams own their domains; leads coordinate, not control.
* **Open Participation:** Clear paths exist for anyone to contribute and grow.
* **Sustainable Pace:** We share the load and rotate responsibilities.

## 3. Contributor Ladder
Our contributor ladder provides a transparent, predictable path for community members to grow their impact and receive formal recognition.

This ladder is aligned with the [CNCF Community Groups program](https://github.com/cncf/communitygroups/#how-to-apply). For the official CNCF process to apply as a volunteer or organizer, see [github.com/cncf/communitygroups](https://github.com/cncf/communitygroups/#how-to-apply).

### Attendees
Everyone who participates in our events.
* **Expectations:**
  * Follow the CNCF Code of Conduct.
  * Respect fellow community members.

### Volunteers
Community members who help with specific tasks or events.
* **How to become one:** 
  * Express interest to any organizer.
  * Join our meetups (in person or virtually) consistently over a period of time, OR actively support multiple consecutive events.
* **Expectations:**
  * Complete committed tasks or communicate early if blocked.
  * Follow guidance from team leads.
  * Help create a welcoming environment at events.
* **Benefits:**
  * Recognition in event communications.
  * Formal path to becoming an organizer.

### Team Leads
Organizers or highly active volunteers who coordinate a functional team.
* **How to become one:** Volunteer or be nominated within your team, confirmed by team consensus.
* **Term:** a set period (e.g., 1 year), with the option to rotate or continue.
* **Expectations:**
  * Coordinate team activities and meetings.
  * Ensure tasks are assigned and tracked.
  * Communicate blockers early to other leads.
  * Mentor team members and delegate effectively.
  * Participate in cross-team coordination.
* **Stepping Down:** Provide reasonable notice when possible and mentor your replacement during the transition.

### Organizers
The core team responsible for running Cloud Native NYC.
* **How to become one:**
  * Actively support community operations continuously over a sustained period (or multiple consecutive events).
  * Be nominated by an existing organizer.
  * Receive approval by a **simple majority vote** of active organizers. *Anti-Capture Guardrail: This majority must include at least one vote from an organizer who works for a different employer than the candidate.*
* **Expectations:**
  * Join at least one functional team.
  * Attend regular organizer meetings.
  * Actively assist with or attend at least **50% of all events hosted annually** (in-person and virtual).
  * Respond to team communications in a timely fashion, scaling with event proximity (within 24 hours during the week leading up to an event; flexible asynchronous check-ins otherwise).
  * Vote on key decisions when called upon.
* **Benefits:**
  * Decision-making voice in community direction.
  * CNCF organizer recognition and perks (swag, training discounts, KubeCon discounts).
  * CNCF Credly badge for active organizers.
* **Stepping Down:** Notify the group with reasonable notice and help transition active responsibilities.

### Emeritus Status
Former organizers who step down in good standing:
* Recognized for past contributions.
* May return to active status through a simplified process.
* Hold no voting rights or responsibilities while emeritus.

## 4. Community Ecosystem Perks (The "Win-Win")
To drive robust engagement, we offer explicit benefits for distinct roles within our chapter. The full list of CNCF Community Groups benefits is available at [github.com/cncf/communitygroups/#benefits-of-joining-the-cncf-community-groups-program](https://github.com/cncf/communitygroups/#benefits-of-joining-the-cncf-community-groups-program).
* **Students & Engineers:** Expand your portfolio, gain mentorship, and access technical talent pipelines.
* **Tech Leads & Organizers:** Priority slots for community keynotes and tech talks (maintaining vendor neutrality standards).
* **Sponsoring Organizations:** Direct alignment with Sponsorships and Venues teams to coordinate hackathons, prioritize venue hosting, and streamline brand visibility for tech hiring.
* **Certification Vouchers:** As an active year-round chapter, we receive 5 free Linux Foundation certification vouchers (CKAD/KCNA) annually. These are distributed among active team members based on merit and event support metrics.

## 5. Functional Teams
Each team owns a domain of community operations. Organizers join teams based on interest and availability. An organizer may serve on multiple teams.

* **Venues Team:** Scout and secure event locations; manage venue relationships and logistics; coordinate accessibility requirements.
* **Sponsorships Team:** Develop and maintain sponsor relationships; create sponsorship packages; ensure sponsor deliverables are met.
* **Speakers Team:** Maintain speaker pipeline and outreach; curate session content and formats; support speakers before and during events.
* **Communications Team:** Manage social media and mailing lists; create event announcements and recaps; handle community marketing.
* **Event Operations Team:** Plan and execute event-day logistics; coordinate volunteers for events; manage registration and check-in.

## 6. Decision Making
We prefer lazy consensus and only move to formal votes when consensus cannot be reached or when the decision type requires it.

### Decision Types
| Type | Method | Minimum Period | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Routine** | Lazy consensus (silence = approval) | 48 hours | Event dates, speaker selection, venue choice, social media content |
| **Standard** | Simple majority vote | 7 days | Team structure changes, event format changes, tooling/platform changes, budget allocation |
| **Significant** | Supermajority (2/3) vote | 7 days | Governance document changes, vendor neutrality exceptions |
| **Critical** | Unanimous consent | 14 days | Code of conduct changes, community dissolution, merging with another community |

*Note: Adding/Removing Organizers is a "Significant" action but uniquely uses a Simple Majority + Cross-Employer Guardrail to prevent corporate stalling.*

### How to Categorize a Decision
* **Routine:** Day-to-day choices that don't set precedent — low stakes, easily reversible.
* **Standard:** Decisions about how we work — processes, structure, tools that can be revisited.
* **Significant:** Decisions about fundamental rules — changes to the core policies.
* **Critical:** Decisions about who we are — community identity, values, or existence.

### Voting Process
All formal votes are conducted via GitHub Issues for transparency:
1. **Open an Issue:** Create an issue in the community GitHub repo using the vote template.
2. **Notify in Slack:** Post a link to the issue in the organizer Slack channel with `@channel`.
3. **Discussion Period:** Discussion happens in the GitHub issue (duration per table above).
4. **Call the Vote:** Proposer updates the issue to call for votes and pings `@channel` in Slack.
5. **Voting Period:** Organizers vote via emoji reactions or comments on the issue.
6. **Record Result:** Close issue with outcome documented; link in meeting notes.

**Quorum:** A majority of active organizers must participate for a vote to be valid.

## 7. Meetings
* **Organizer Meetings:** Monthly (or as needed before events). Purpose: Cross-team coordination, decisions, planning. Notes shared with all organizers within 48 hours.
* **Team Meetings:** As determined by each team. Purpose: Team-specific planning and execution.

## 8. Removing Organizers
Organizers hold a position of trust. They may be removed for:
* Prolonged inactivity (unable to assist with 50% of annual events, or unavailable for 90+ days without notice).
* Code of Conduct violations.
* Vendor neutrality violations.

**Process:**
1. A GitHub issue is raised tagging the individual (per CNCF rules). They have a strict 30-day window to respond.
2. The issue is discussed privately with the individual first.
3. If unresolved, it is brought to the organizer group.
4. A simple majority vote of the remaining organizers is required for removal.

## 9. Vendor Neutrality
Per CNCF guidelines:
* No more than 50% of organizers may be from the same company.
* Content and speakers should represent diverse viewpoints.
* Sponsors receive recognition, not editorial control.
* Organizers act in the community's interest, not their employer's interest.

## 10. Code of Conduct
All community activities follow the CNCF Code of Conduct.
* **Reporting:** Concerns may be reported to any organizer or directly to `conduct@cncf.io`.

## 11. Activity Requirements
Per CNCF Community Groups program:
* Host at least one event every 90 days.
* Maintain a minimum of 2 active organizers.
* Respond to CNCF communications.
